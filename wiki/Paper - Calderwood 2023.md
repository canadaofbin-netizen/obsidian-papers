# Core Summary
This paper introduces the concept of *extra-normative work*—work performed outside the typical time and/or space expectations of a job role (e.g., after-hours work, abrupt teleworking during COVID-19). The authors apply *situational strength theory* to understand how this work impacts employee strain. Situational strength consists of four facets: consistency, clarity, consequences, and constraints. 
The studies demonstrate a paradox: while overall "strong" extra-normative situations increase strain, a facet-level analysis reveals diverging effects. Clarity and consistency in extra-normative work actually *reduce* employee strain (by mitigating ambiguity), whereas constraints and severe consequences *enhance* strain (by undermining autonomy and inducing anxiety).

# Radical Theoretical Lens: Cognitive Thermodynamics and Entropic Strain
Standard organizational psychology views strain through role theory and job demands-resources models. A radical alternative is to view extra-normative work through the lens of **Cognitive Thermodynamics**. 

In this framework, the normative work role is a low-entropy, highly ordered cognitive state space. Extra-normative work acts as an exogenous thermal perturbation, rapidly expanding the state space of potential behaviors and drastically increasing cognitive entropy. The employee's brain must expend immense metabolic energy to navigate this expanded, uncertain space, manifesting phenomenologically as "strain."

Under this thermodynamic lens, the facets of situational strength operate differently:
* **Clarity and Consistency (Maxwell's Demons):** These act as information-theoretic signals that collapse the expanded state space without requiring the employee's metabolic expenditure. By providing free information, they act like Maxwell's Demon, sorting the chaotic state space back into a low-entropy configuration, thereby reducing the metabolic burn rate (strain).
* **Constraints and Consequences (Rigid Boundary Friction):** Rather than providing information to collapse uncertainty, these act as rigid, high-pressure boundary conditions on the expanded state space. They force the cognitive system to avoid certain microstates via penalty (consequences) or structural compression (constraints). This forced compression generates psychological "friction" and heat, accelerating metabolic depletion and leading directly to emotional exhaustion and anxiety. 

Thus, strain is not merely a reaction to psychological demands; it is the physical thermodynamic cost of managing cognitive entropy. Extra-normative work requires organizations to supply "informational cooling" (clarity/consistency) rather than "mechanical compression" (constraints/consequences) to prevent systemic thermodynamic failure (burnout).
