# Meyer et al. (2009) Situational Strength as a Moderator of the Relationship Between Personality and Performance

## Hypotheses
- **Hypothesis 1:** The conscientiousness–performance relationship is stronger in occupations low in constraints than in occupations high in constraints.
- **Hypothesis 2:** The conscientiousness–performance relationship is stronger in occupations low in consequences than in occupations high in consequences.

## Methodology
- **Study Design:** Meta-analysis of primary empirical studies.
- **Sample:** 114 primary studies representing 13 of the U.S. Department of Labor's 23 Standard Occupational Classification codes, yielding 162 independent correlations and a total of 34,659 employed participants.
- **Inclusion Criteria:** Studies had to include a correlation between conscientiousness and individual job performance calculated using actual employees (not students), all participants in the study sample had to share the same occupation, and the sample size had to be reported. 
- **Operationalization of Moderators:** Occupational Information Network (O*NET) version 11.0 was used to derive numeric estimates of occupation-level situational strength. 14 items from O*NET's "generalized work activities" and "work context" sub-categories were coded into two broad facets: "constraints" (e.g., freedom to make decisions) and "consequences" (e.g., responsible for others' health and safety). A composite "global situational strength" was also created.
- **Analysis:** Modified weighted least squares (WLS) regression equations were used to test if constraints, consequences, and global situational strength significantly moderated the conscientiousness–performance correlation coefficients.

## Empirical Findings
- **Main Effects:** Mean uncorrected correlations for conscientiousness with job performance were $r = .15$ for overall performance, $r = .13$ for task performance, and $r = .16$ for contextual performance.
- **Moderating Effects of Constraints:** Constraints significantly moderated the conscientiousness–overall performance relationship ($\beta = -.16, p < .05$) and marginally moderated the conscientiousness–task performance relationship ($\beta = -.16, p < .10$).
- **Moderating Effects of Consequences:** Consequences significantly moderated the conscientiousness–performance relationship for both overall performance ($\beta = -.23, p < .05$) and task performance ($\beta = -.21, p < .05$).
- **Moderating Effects of Global Situational Strength:** Global situational strength significantly moderated the relationships for both overall performance ($\beta = -.26, p < .01$) and task performance ($\beta = -.22, p < .05$).
- **Effect Sizes by Occupation Strength:** Predicted uncorrected conscientiousness–performance correlations ranged from $r = .09$ to $.23$ for overall performance and $r = .06$ to $.18$ for task performance, with the stronger correlations observed in prototypically weak occupations (e.g., barber, telemarketer) compared to prototypically strong occupations (e.g., nuclear equipment operation technician, surgeon).

## Stated Limitations
- The use of O*NET estimates represents average conditions one would expect to find in a "typical" setting within a given occupation. Therefore, the study could not assess the moderating effects of variability in situational strength that exists within the same occupation but in different organizations, nor variability within the same organization at alternative levels of analysis (e.g., work group, or across societies/cultures).

## Core Variables Measured
- **Predictor:** Trait Conscientiousness
- **Criterion:** Job Performance (Task performance, Contextual performance, Overall performance)
- **Moderators:** Occupation-level Situational Strength (Constraints, Consequences, Global situational strength)
