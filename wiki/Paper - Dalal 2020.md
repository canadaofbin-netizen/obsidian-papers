# Paper - Dalal 2020

## Metadata
- **Title:** Extending Situational Strength Theory to Account for Situation-Outcome Mismatch
- **Authors:** Reeshad S. Dalal, Balca Alaybek, Zitong Sheng, Samantha J. Holland, Alan J. Tomassetti
- **Year:** 2020
- **Journal:** Journal of Business and Psychology

## Core Summary
The paper addresses a purported anomaly in situational strength theory: the finding that "strong" situations (which typically constrain behavior and weaken the expression of personality traits) can sometimes *amplify* the effect of personality on counterproductive work behavior (CWB). Dalal et al. resolve this by introducing the concept of a "situation-outcome mismatch." When a strong situation successfully blocks one outlet for poor performance (e.g., forcing an unconscientious employee to maintain high task performance), the employee experiences negative affect. Because their natural behavioral predisposition is suppressed, they substitute or "displace" this tendency into a secondary outlet, such as CWB. This displacement effectively strengthens the link between their personality and CWB, unless that secondary outlet is also explicitly blocked by a strong, CWB-focused situation.

## Radical Theoretical Lens: Thermodynamic Conservation of Behavioral Entropy

Instead of traditional psychological constructs like "displacement" or "reactance," this phenomenon can be modeled through the lens of **Thermodynamic Conservation of Behavioral Entropy**. 

In this framework, personality traits (such as low conscientiousness or low agreeableness) dictate a baseline level of "behavioral entropy"—a natural tendency toward disorder or variance in output. An organization attempting to enforce a "strong situation" (e.g., strict task performance metrics) is acting as a rigid pressure vessel, applying systemic energy to artificially lower the local entropy of the employee's task-related behaviors. 

However, following the analogue of the First Law of Thermodynamics, this entropic energy cannot simply be destroyed by managerial constraints; it must be conserved. When the primary behavioral vector (task performance) is compressed, the system experiences a buildup of internal pressure, experienced cognitively and physiologically as "negative affect" (the thermodynamic equivalent of heat friction). 

Because the strong situation only sealed one specific outlet, the conserved behavioral entropy seeks the path of least resistance. It undergoes a "phase transition" and leaks out through unconstrained, orthogonal dimensions of the organizational topology—manifesting as Counterproductive Work Behavior (CWB). Thus, CWB is not a separate behavioral decision, but rather the necessary energetic exhaust of a high-entropy individual trapped in a low-entropy containment field. To stop CWB without creating a new leak, the organization would theoretically need to exert absolute pressure across all behavioral dimensions (a total vacuum state), which would likely lead to catastrophic systemic failure (e.g., total burnout or turnover) rather than compliance.
