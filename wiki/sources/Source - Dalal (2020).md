# Source Note: Dalal (2020)

## Meta Information
- **Title**: Extending Situational Strength Theory to Account for Situation-Outcome Mismatch
- **Authors**: Reeshad S. Dalal, Balca Alaybek, Zitong Sheng, Samantha J. Holland, Alan J. Tomassetti
- **Year**: 2020

## Core Argument & Summary
The authors extend situational strength theory to explain previously anomalous findings where "strong" situations actually amplified the impact of personality on poor performance. They argue that when a strong situation blocks one outlet for poor performance (e.g., low task performance), employees who are predisposed to such behavior experience negative affect. Consequently, they displace this behavioral tendency to a second, mismatched outlet (e.g., counterproductive work behavior - CWB). Thus, a strong situation targeting one domain can inadvertently strengthen the expression of negative personality traits in another domain, unless that second domain is also restricted by its own strong situation.

## Key Variables
- **Predictors (Personality Traits)**: Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Emotional Stability
- **Dependent Variables (Outcomes)**: Counterproductive Work Behavior (CWB) - specifically, Organizationally directed (CWBO) and Interpersonally directed (CWBI)
- **Moderators (Situational Strength)**: Task-performance-focused situational strength (facets: clarity and constraints); CWB-focused situational strength (organizational intolerance of CWB)
- **Mediator**: Negative Affect

## Methodology
- **Design**: A two-wave longitudinal survey design separated by a 2-week interval to reduce common-method bias.
- **Sample**: 369 working adults (from the USA and India) recruited via Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk).
- **Analysis**: Dual-stage moderated mediation analysis using the PROCESS macro in SPSS.

## Specific Findings related to 'Situational Strength'
1. **Situation-Outcome Match (Conventional Prediction)**: *CWB-focused situational strength* significantly weakened the negative relationship between all three personality traits and CWB. When the situation's strength cues matched the outcome domain (i.e., strict intolerance of CWB), personality mattered less, supporting classic situational strength theory.
2. **Situation-Outcome Mismatch (Displacement Effect)**: *Task-performance-focused situational strength* unexpectedly strengthened the negative relationship between personality (specifically agreeableness and conscientiousness) and CWB. When employees were constrained by strong task-performance rules, their predisposition toward poor behavior was channeled into higher CWB instead.
3. **Mediation by Negative Affect**: The process driving the mismatch effect is mediated by negative affect. Employees predisposed to low performance experienced higher negative affect when blocked by strong task-performance cues, which then drove their engagement in CWB.
4. **Dual-stage Moderation**: This indirect displacement pathway (via negative affect) was successfully cut off/weakened only when *CWB-focused situational strength* was also high. 
5. **Trait Differences**: These findings held strongly for conscientiousness and agreeableness, but there was little support for emotional stability, suggesting situational strength interacts most robustly with traits that have the highest baseline relationships with the targeted job performance criteria.
