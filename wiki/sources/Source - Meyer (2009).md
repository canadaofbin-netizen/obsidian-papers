# Source Note: Meyer (2009)

## Meta Information
- **Title**: A meta-analytic investigation into the moderating effects of situational strength on the conscientiousness–performance relationship
- **Authors**: Rustin D. Meyer, Reeshad S. Dalal, and Silvia Bonaccio
- **Year**: 2009

## Core Argument & Summary
The study uses meta-analysis to demonstrate that occupation-level **situational strength** is a significant source of variability in how well trait conscientiousness predicts job performance. The central argument is that the conscientiousness–performance relationship is stronger in "weak" occupations (where employees have high behavioral and decisional latitude) compared to "strong" occupations (where behavior is highly restricted by rules or significant consequences). In strong situations, situational cues dictate behavior for all employees, neutralizing the natural performance advantage typically held by highly conscientious individuals. 

## Key Variables
- **Predictor Variable**: Trait Conscientiousness.
- **Criterion Variable (Outcome)**: Job Performance (specifically analyzed across Overall Performance, Task Performance, and Contextual Performance).
- **Moderator Variable**: Occupation-level Situational Strength, which was further broken down into:
    - **Constraints**: The amount of behavioral/decisional restriction placed on an employee (or conversely, the amount of autonomy/latitude).
    - **Consequences**: The presence of contingencies between an employee's decisions/behaviors and the outcomes accruing to themselves, others, the organization, or external stakeholders.
    - **Global Situational Strength**: A composite formative construct of both constraints and consequences.

## Methodology
- **Meta-Analysis**: The researchers aggregated 114 primary studies (yielding 162 independent correlations and N = 34,659 participants) that reported uncorrected correlations between conscientiousness and individual job performance.
- **O\*NET Database**: They used the Occupational Information Network (O\*NET) to quantitatively operationalize occupation-level situational strength. They mapped the occupations from the primary studies to O\*NET occupational units and extracted scores for "constraints" and "consequences."
- **Analytic Procedure**: They employed Weighted Least Squares (WLS) regression to test if constraints, consequences, and global situational strength moderated the conscientiousness–performance correlation coefficients.

## Specific Findings Related to 'Situational Strength'
- **Moderation Effects**: The results confirmed that situational strength negatively moderates the criterion-related validity of conscientiousness. As occupation-level situational strength increases, the magnitude of the conscientiousness-performance relationship decreases. 
- **Effect Sizes**: Predicted uncorrected conscientiousness–performance correlations ranged from *r* = .09 to .23 for overall performance, and *r* = .06 to .18 for task performance, with the stronger correlations consistently observed in weak occupations.
- **Constraints**: High constraints significantly reduced the conscientiousness-overall performance relationship (β = -.16, p < .05) and marginally reduced it for task performance (β = -.16, p < .10).
- **Consequences**: High consequences significantly weakened the relationship for both overall performance (β = -.23, p < .05) and task performance (β = -.21, p < .05).
- **Global Situational Strength**: Global situational strength had a significant moderating effect for both overall performance (β = -.26, p < .01) and task performance (β = -.22, p < .05). For example, in strong occupations like "nuclear equipment operation technician", the correlation was around *r* = .09, whereas in weak occupations like "barber", it rose to *r* = .23.
